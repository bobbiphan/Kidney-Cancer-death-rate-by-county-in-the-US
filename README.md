Geographic Pattern of Kidney Cancer Mortality in the United States

The map highlights U.S. counties in the top 10% of kidney cancer death rates. A clear spatial pattern emerges: many of the highest-mortality counties cluster in the central region of the United States, particularly across the Midwest and parts of the Great Plains. Several factors may contribute to this geographic concentration.

One possible explanation is the urban–rural healthcare divide. Many counties in the middle of the country are rural and have fewer medical specialists and healthcare resources. Research has shown that rural counties often have higher cancer mortality despite lower incidence, partly because cancers are diagnosed later and access to specialized care such as urologists is limited. Lower physician density and fewer screening opportunities can lead to delayed treatment and poorer outcomes.  ￼

Another contributing factor may be differences in lifestyle and health risk factors. Kidney cancer mortality is associated with risk factors such as smoking, obesity, hypertension, and diabetes. These conditions are more prevalent in some Midwestern and rural populations, which may increase mortality rates in these areas. Regional variation in these risk factors can produce clusters of higher cancer death rates.  ￼

Environmental and demographic factors may also play a role. Historical research has identified clusters of kidney cancer mortality in the north-central United States, potentially linked to environmental exposures or population characteristics, including ethnic and socioeconomic patterns.  ￼

Finally, broader public-health disparities between rural and urban communities may influence outcomes. In rural America, cancer death rates generally decline more slowly than in urban areas due to structural factors such as limited prevention programs, fewer hospitals, and barriers to timely treatment.  ￼

Overall, the concentration of high kidney cancer mortality in the middle of the country likely reflects a combination of healthcare access, lifestyle risk factors, and regional demographic or environmental influences, rather than a single cause. Further analysis incorporating variables such as rurality, healthcare availability, and socioeconomic indicators could help better explain these spatial patterns.
